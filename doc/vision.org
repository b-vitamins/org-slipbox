#+title: org-slipbox Vision

* North Star

`org-slipbox` should become a serious Org-based slip-box: a personal
knowledge system that helps its user think, not merely store notes.

The model is not a pile of hyperlinked documents and not a miniature wiki. A
slip-box is useful when it becomes a competent partner in thought: something
that helps surface connections, tensions, absences, trails, and lines of
development that the user did not already have fully in view.

* Product Thesis

- Org files remain the source of truth.
- The index remains derived and disposable.
- Interactive work must stay cheap at large corpus sizes.
- The Emacs front-end must stay thin, explicit, and comprehensible.
- The system must talk back through grounded structure, not through vague
  theatrics.

* What "Conversation Partner" Means

The system should not stop at answering "what links here?" It should also help
answer questions such as:

- What else does this note make thinkable?
- What nearby lines of thought are present but weakly connected?
- What ideas keep recurring across otherwise distant regions?
- What unresolved or contradictory material is accumulating around this topic?
- What has gone dormant but now bears on the current line of work?

This requires:

- explainable relatedness, not just opaque ranking
- multiple ways to pivot through the corpus
- trails, comparison, and backtracking
- support for surprise without sacrificing trust

* Buffer As Cockpit

The current-node buffer should mature into the main exploratory surface of the
system.

The persistent buffer should remain cheap and minimal. It exists to keep
context close at hand without making ordinary editing expensive.

The dedicated buffer is already the richer cockpit, and `0.5.x` should treat
its basic exploration model as settled: declared lenses, engine-backed
explanations, factual comparison, and explicit trails. It should support:

- indexed neighborhoods beyond direct backlinks
- explicit pivots between lenses such as refs, time, tasks, bridges, dormant
  material, unresolved material, and structural proximity
- explicit trails, comparisons, backtracking, and detached branching now, with
  durable saved artifacts extracted from that settled model and `0.7.x`
  turning them into the first usable headless workbench, and `0.8.x`
  composing named workflows, corpus-health audits, bounded discovery, report
  outputs, and stricter scale gates over that same model, and `0.9.x` making
  repeated workflow and audit loops durable, reviewable, and diffable without
  turning review records into notes, saved exploration artifacts, or broad
  automated mutation
- clear explanations for why a node or section is being shown
- controlled discovery of non-obvious but relevant material through indexed
  evidence over refs, tasks, time, and local graph structure

* One Exploratory Model, Two Surfaces

`org-slipbox` should not grow one model for the Emacs cockpit and another for
headless or programmable use.

The dedicated buffer is the interactive realization of the exploratory model.
The native programmable workbench is the task-shaped, non-UI realization of
that same model.

The cockpit should feel settled before broader headless workbench extraction
hardens any later public surface.
The first extraction step is now durable artifacts for saved views, trails, and
comparisons plus a narrow machine-facing surface for save, inspect, list,
execute, and delete.
`0.7.x` turns that narrow surface into the first usable headless
workbench through task-shaped resolve, live explore, compare, and
artifact-lifecycle operations over the canonical daemon boundary, with
JSON-first machine use but without pretending the whole programmable platform
is complete.
`0.8.x` broadens that workbench by composition rather than by transport
sprawl: named workflows, corpus-health audits, bounded workflow discovery from
configured directories, report output surfaces, and stricter scale guarantees
over the same settled exploratory model.
`0.9.x` makes that composed workbench operational: durable review runs, review
status, review diffs, and read-only remediation previews for supported audit
findings. Review state is a distinct domain concept. It must not collapse into
ordinary note identity, saved exploration-artifact identity, task-manager
sprawl, or broad write automation.
Broader extension, plugin-runtime, MCP, and platform surfaces still come
later.

This implies:

- lenses mean the same thing in the cockpit and in headless workflows
- explanations come from the engine rather than ad hoc frontend reconstruction
- trails, comparisons, saved views, and later workbench artifacts are durable
  domain concepts, not buffer accidents
- review runs, review status, and review diffs are durable workbench concepts,
  but remain distinct from both notes and saved exploration artifacts
- programmable surfaces expose meaningful operations rather than transport guts
- the workbench should be extracted from a proven exploratory model, not
  invented in parallel and allowed to drift

* Architectural Commitments

- Parsing, indexing, ranking, query execution, and file mutation stay in Rust.
- Emacs Lisp owns commands, session state, and presentation only.
- Modes own hooks. Loading the package must not mutate user state.
- File-level updates stay incremental and explicit.
- Expensive discovery stays off persistent redisplay paths.
- The persistent buffer stays cheap by design; richer discovery belongs in the
  dedicated buffer and later headless workbench surfaces.
- Growth must happen by adding focused modules behind stable seams, not by
  swelling central files.

* Product Standards

- No parity theatre. If a workflow is claimed, it must work.
- No convenience shortcuts that compromise correctness or scale.
- No hidden global state deciding whether the system is right.
- No broad feature churn without a clear fit to the slip-box thesis.
- No UI richness that pushes hot-path work back into Emacs Lisp.

* Anti-Goals

- Not a clone of social knowledge graphs.
- Not a generic note app with fashionable graph visuals.
- Not a mini encyclopedia maintained for its own sake.
- Not a chatbot pasted on top of an index.
- Not a system whose apparent intelligence depends on stale caches or ambient
  editor state.

* Measure Of Success

`org-slipbox` is succeeding when:

- it remains fast and predictable as corpora become large
- it remains easy to package and integrate in ordinary Emacs environments
- its exploratory surface helps users discover structure they did not manually
  encode ahead of time
- new features deepen the conversation-partner model rather than dilute it
